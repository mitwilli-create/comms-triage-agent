Technical Roadmap Report (TRR) Principles
1. Introduction
The Problem
Infrastructure fragmentation at the organization often persists not due to a lack of technical skill, but because of good-faith disagreements between experts on which solution is best (TRR Principles).
What is a TRR?
A Technical Roadmap Report (TRR) is a document designed to propose a plan to solve a specific cross-organizational technical problem which doesn’t already have a well-established solution (TRR Principles). The report functions by:

	•	Evaluating the available options for solving the problem (TRR Principles).
	•	Choosing clear recommendations (TRR Principles).
	•	Including next steps for addressing uncertainties, such as determining feasibility and cost-effectiveness (TRR Principles).


2. Core Principles of Decision Making
The process of making decisions is as critical as the decisions themselves (TRR Principles). The TRR framework adopts four core principles from [INTERNAL_LINK]:roadmaps:

	•	Focus on Execution: Excellent execution can turn imperfect technical decisions into successful products (TRR Principles).
	•	Involve Stakeholders: Early stakeholder involvement and feedback is essential in producing findings and robust decisions (TRR Principles).
	•	Bring Experts: Experts should be drawn from across the company, not just from within the specific Domain membership (TRR Principles).
	•	Transparency: Authors must acknowledge the advantages of the options they didn't choose and the problems associated with the decision chosen (TRR Principles).
Fighting Decision Paralysis
The TRR exists to fight decision paralysis (TRR Principles). It operates on the understanding that reasonable people can disagree on the right direction (TRR Principles), but an imperfect decision is better than no decision (TRR Principles).

	•	Feasibility: The right decision is not always the best technical decision in a “vacuum,” but rather one that is feasible, including operational constraints (TRR Principles).
	•	Objectivity: Authors must be as objective as possible and be aware of their biases (TRR Principles).


3. Deep Dive: Key Pillars
Stakeholder Involvement
While a TRR is “binding,” it does not magically transform the organization from a bottoms-up, consensus-driven company (TRR Principles).

	•	Early Engagement: Stakeholders must be involved early to prevent costly feedback surprises later (TRR Principles).
	•	Insights: Stakeholders will likely have insights you didn’t consider (TRR Principles).
	•	Support: Implementation success is more likely if the stakeholders can support the decision-making process (TRR Principles).
Transparency & Trust
No decision will please everyone, and some groups (e.g., teams behind deprecated infrastructure, clients facing migration costs) will be especially skeptical (TRR Principles). Execution is hampered if people perceive the decision as political or not justified by technical considerations (TRR Principles).

	•	Diligence: Convey the diligence and legitimacy of the decision-making process (TRR Principles).
	•	Honesty: Openly acknowledge the strengths and weaknesses/risks of all plausible alternatives (TRR Principles). A robust "Alternatives Considered" section conveys the care and seriousness of the process (TRR Principles).
	•	Sub-optimal Decisions: Acknowledge if there wasn’t enough information to make the “perfect” decision. A sub-optimal decision is better than no decision (TRR Principles).
	•	No Corp-Speak: Do not dodge sensitive issues or use "writing-by-committee." team members want to be treated like adults rather than “managed” and have sensitive BS meters (TRR Principles).


4. Principles of Effective TRRs
An effective TRR adheres to five specific characteristics:
1. Solves a Problem
	•	Clear Statement: The report must have a clear and aligned problem statement (TRR Principles).
	•	Focus: Avoid ‘boiling the ocean’ by focusing on a small number of specific important problems (TRR Principles).
	•	Impact: Include clear documentation of the impact and benefits of solving the problem (TRR Principles).
	•	Alignment: Ensure early alignment on the problem among stakeholders to avoid a "solution in search of a problem" (TRR Principles).
2. Communicates Direction
TRRs are directional documents and should not 'set in stone' a long term specific plan (TRR Principles).

	•	Domain-Internal Audience: Focus on specific short-term plans, establish feedback channels, and outline long-term plans that are regularly validated with data (TRR Principles).
	•	Domain-External Audience: Set long-term vision and prioritize the overall good to [CORP_NAME] over advantages to individual infrastructure teams (TRR Principles). Provide immediate "well-lit-paths" for affected teams (TRR Principles).
3. Incremental
	•	Quick Iteration: Prioritize work with high ROI and don’t try to predict beyond the next N months.
	•	Agile: Be agile and respond to contingencies; be open to new ideas or data points that may change the strategy (TRR Principles).
	•	Data-Driven: Define metrics to track improvements and costs, and establish guidance for when further investment is no longer worth the cost (TRR Principles).
4. Works Well Today
Any guidance from the TRR to the organization must be proven to work well today (TRR Principles).

	•	Actionable Items: Focus on specifics, immediate ownership, and required changes/headcount (TRR Principles).
	•	Automation First: Don’t ask for migrations before automation has been attempted (TRR Principles).
	•	Controls: Ensure controls are in place to prevent regression (e.g., defunding, drop-of-support) and to ‘stop the bleeding’ regarding disallowed infrastructure (TRR Principles).
5. Standard Process
The standard Churn Policy ([INTERNAL_LINK]) still applies (TRR Principles).

	•	Exceptions: Exceptions to distribute more work must go through the standard process (TRR Principles).
	•	Alignment: Some ICM requirements (Exec sponsor, Roadmap alignment) are already addressed as part of the TRR process (TRR Principles).


5. Summary: What a TRR Is and Is Not
A TRR Is...
A TRR Is Not...
A collaborative approach to make a roadmap decision on a specific cross-organizational technical problem (TRR Principles).
A declaration of the desired end state without a rough plan to get there (TRR Principles).
Resulting in a clear, cross-organizational decision with follow-on ownership (TRR Principles).
A fully-detailed 3-year plan (TRR Principles).
Based on stakeholder engagement, feasibility, and ROI analysis (TRR Principles).
A detailed accounting of the cost of adopting the solution (TRR Principles).
