Smart Brevity Rules — Internal Comms Agent Knowledge Base
Purpose: This document teaches the agent how to revise internal communications using Smart Brevity methodology, and how to explain each edit to AI-skeptical users.

Core Principle: Every revision must include a rationale. Users need to understand WHY a change was made, not just WHAT changed.


1. The Smart Brevity Framework
Every communication follows this structure:

Element

Constraint

Grab attention, teach something

The Logic: Readers ask two questions within milliseconds:

	•	What is this about?
	•	Is it relevant to me?

If they can't answer "yes" immediately, they're gone.


2. Headline/Subject Line Rules
Requirements
	•	Length: 10 words or fewer (60 characters max)
	•	Specificity: Must teach something concrete
	•	Tone: Conversational, not corporate
	•	Format: Declarative statements, not questions

3. First Sentence ("What's New") Rules
The Barstool Test
Imagine you're sprinting to catch your VP before an elevator closes. You have 3 seconds to share critical news. What would you blurt out?

That's your first sentence.
Requirements
	•	ONE sentence only — Forces clarity on what matters most
	•	Most important detail first — Not chronological, not background
	•	Conversational — The same words you'd say out loud
	•	Distinct from headline — Build on it, don't repeat it
Common Failures
Problem
Example

4. "Why It Matters" Rules
Requirements
	•	Reader-centric — Not why it matters to YOU, why it matters to THEM
	•	1-2 sentences maximum
	•	Concrete impact — What changes for them specifically?
Audience Calibration
The same news requires different "Why It Matters" for different audiences:

Audience
News: "New Green Partnership Signed"
Executive
"This brings us 10% closer to our H2 revenue target."
Team Lead
"We'll need to expand hiring — we're now understaffed for this workload."
External/Press
"This advances our commitment to net zero by 2050."


5. Scannability Rules
Fact: 60-80% of readers scan, not read.
Visual Techniques
	•	Short paragraphs — 1-3 sentences max
	•	White space — Breaks readers out of scanning fog
	•	Bold axioms — "Why it matters:" / "What's next:" / "The bottom line:"
	•	Bullets — Only for genuinely parallel items (not as a crutch)
Axioms to Use

When to Use

	•	Somewhat, relatively, fairly

8. Before/After Examples
Example 1: Email Announcement
BEFORE (247 words):

Subject: Update on our plans

Hello all,

I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to take a moment to reach out to provide an update on our plans for the rest of the year. As you know, we've been closely monitoring the evolving situation, and after much deliberation and consultation with various stakeholders across the organization, we've made some decisions that I think are important to share with everyone.

Given the uncertainty around us and the feedback we've received from team members about their preferences and concerns, we've decided that it would be best to continue with our current flexible approach. We believe this is the right decision for our organization at this time...

AFTER (52 words):

Subject: Remote work extended through 2024

Everyone can work from home for the rest of 2024.

Why it matters: You don't need to make childcare or commuting changes — current arrangements can continue.

What's next: Managers will schedule optional in-person team days. Watch for calendar invites by Friday.

Reduction: 79%


Example 2: Project Status Update
BEFORE (189 words):

Subject: CS v CF Comps and wanted Input for next steps on CF

Hello all,

I think it is time for our team to revisit our cookie vendor policy. As you know cookies are an important part of our day-to-day operations. I believe we last spoke about comparing our current cookie vendor CS, last semester, I could be wrong but let me know if I am misremembering. Anyway, last semester Elmo asked us to review this potential new cookie vendor...

[continues with extensive background]

AFTER (67 words):

Subject: Cookie vendor trial — need 5 volunteers

CookieFinder is offering us a second trial after fixing the issues we flagged.

Why it matters: If CF now works, we could get AI-powered vendor matching — something CS doesn't offer.

What's next: Reply by Friday if you want to join the 15-person trial (first trial participants preferred).

Reduction: 65%

10. Quality Checklist
Before delivering any revision, verify:
Headline/Subject
	•	≤10 words / 60 characters?
	•	Teaches something specific?
	•	Conversational, not corporate?
First Sentence
	•	ONE sentence only?
	•	Contains the most important detail?
	•	Different from (not repeating) headline?
Why It Matters
	•	Written from READER's perspective?
	•	1-2 sentences max?
	•	Concrete impact stated?
Overall
	•	30-50% shorter than original?
	•	All filler phrases removed?
	•	Active voice throughout?
	•	Clear action item (if applicable)?
	•	Every edit has a rationale?


11. Edge Cases
When NOT to Cut
	•	Legal/compliance language — Flag for human review, don't rewrite
	•	Technical specifications — Precision matters more than brevity
	•	Sensitive personnel matters — Nuance may be intentional
	•	Direct quotes from leadership — Don't alter without approval
When to Escalate
If the original content has any of these, escalate rather than revise autonomously:

	•	Legal implications
	•	HR/personnel sensitivity
	•	Executive-level announcements
	•	External/public-facing distribution
	•	Crisis communications
	•	Anything where tone miscalibration could cause harm
